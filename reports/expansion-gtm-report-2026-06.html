Where to put the money · Who to talk to · How to get out in the world
Prepared June 11, 2026 · HD Productions LLC · Companion to the Expansion Idea Bank and Portfolio Audit
The one-sentence strategy: Borrow trusted audiences (churches, schools, dealerships, creators), convert every bit of attention into the email list, and let TikTok and Etsy carry the discovery layer. The three B2B ideas are all the same move — somebody else's trust, our product — and that is the cheapest customer acquisition available.

1. Where to Put the Money

Rule zero: free fixes before funded bets

  • Wire the email-capture endpoint (signups are currently dropped)
  • Attach analytics (framework already built into the site)
  • Verify live Stripe mode and order-email configuration in production

The allocation — first ~$5,000 of expansion capital

ShareInvestmentWhy this order
40% (~$2,000)Production capacity — printer #2, filament stock, assembly jigs, packaging; two outsource quotes as the scale planManual fulfillment caps ~10–20 units/week. Every channel dies at that ceiling. Capacity is the only investment that multiplies all the others.
25% (~$1,250)Content — one pro photo day (every kit on real cars) + 20–30 short vertical videosThe tile snap is inherently satisfying — made for Reels/TikTok. One spend feeds the site, ads, Etsy, wholesale pitches, and influencer briefs.
20% (~$1,000)Seeding & samples — ~50 free frames: 25 to micro-influencers, 25 as B2B samplesOne free unit to the right creator can outperform months of ads. A $12-cost sample that lands a 50-unit church order is the best ROI in this plan.
15% (~$750)Paid ads, seasonal windows only — $25/day bursts: pre–July 4, September, early October, Black FridayAds amplify proven buying windows; they do not create demand. Never run them outside a deadline season.

Development (code) investment: almost none needed

  • Group-order / bulk flow — one feature unlocks all three B2B channels: a shareable per-organization order page with a group code (school fundraisers, church youth groups, dealer bulk pricing).
  • Kit activation — Game Day, Halloween, and Christmas kits are config flips plus photography; the designs already exist.
  • Not yet: subscriptions, the public designer, custom-logo tooling — all real, all premature until 2–3 drops prove repeat purchase.

What NOT to fund yet

  • Licensing (NASCAR, colleges, pro sports) — capital-heavy and slow; earn the volume first
  • Inventory for more than two unproven kits at once — activate sequentially, read sell-through
  • Retail storefronts or trade-show booths — wholesale via Faire reaches shops cheaper

2. Who to Talk To — Exact Roles

Churches (identity + channel in one)

  • Youth pastor / youth director — the single highest-leverage contact in this report. Owns fundraisers and trunk-or-treat: one yes = a faith-kit fundraiser + an October event for the Halloween kit + the congregation newsletter.
  • Church administrator / bookstore manager — consignment or wholesale at larger churches.
  • Where to start: the 10 largest STL-metro congregations plus the warm network.
  • Pitch: “A made-in-St-Louis fundraiser your families keep on their cars — schools sell candy bars; you sell something people see every day.”

Schools

  • PTO/PTA president and booster club treasurer — they choose fall fundraisers in July–August; this outreach cannot wait.
  • Athletic director — spirit-wear angle for fall sports.
  • Pitch: school keeps $10–15/unit; durable, not consumable; every sold frame is a rolling ad in the pickup line.

Car dealerships

  • Used-car independents first (the owner decides, one meeting), then franchise stores via the parts & accessories manager or F&I manager.
  • Pitch: “You already bolt a branded frame on every car you sell. This one the customer keeps, plays with, and shows off — with your name on the rail.”
  • Pilot: 5 STL dealerships, free 10-frame sample program, $10–14/frame at volume.

Local distribution & press (the made-in-STL story)

  • STL boutique gift-shop buyers, plus Faire (wholesale marketplace) for nationwide indie-shop discovery.
  • Post-Dispatch business desk; KSDK / Fox2 lifestyle; STL Magazine gift guides — “St. Louis crew launches snap-on frame for America’s 250th, capped at 250” is a pitchable story. Gift-guide pitches go out in September.
  • Cars & Coffee organizers, Cardinals tailgate lots — hand out frames, film it.

Creators (seed, don’t sponsor)

  • Car TikTok/IG micro-creators (10–100K) — the snap/swap demo IS the content. Free kits, no strings.
  • Dog-mom / pet lifestyle creators — primes the Pet kit before it exists.
  • Trunk-or-treat / mom-content creators — September seeding for October buzz.
  • Uncommon Goods & The Grommet submission portals — apply NOW; holiday lead times run 3–6 months.

3. How to Get Out in the World

Owned — the asset that compounds

  • The email list is the business. Every kit drop goes to the list first (“Founding members get 48 hours early”). Target: 1,000 emails by Labor Day via the QR loop and giveaways.
  • QR viral loop — every frame on the road links back to the designer; add a referral hook later (“10 scans = $5 off your next set”).
  • The site — one landing page per kit drop, plus /fundraisers and /dealers as the B2B front doors.

Rented — where attention lives

  • TikTok + IG Reels — the one rented channel to take seriously: visual, tactile, transforming product = algorithm food. 3–5 posts/week from the content-day footage; every post funnels to email.
  • Etsy — list immediately; standing search demand for custom plate frames and made-in-USA gifts.
  • Amazon FBA — later (Q4 at earliest); ~45% margin hit needs organic proof and capacity headroom first.

Borrowed — the cheap growth

  • The B2B channels ARE borrowed audiences: a congregation, a parent list, a dealership’s customer flow — trust no ad can buy. The group-order build converts them.
  • Creator seeding and local press per Section 2.
  • Convert all borrowed attention to owned: every fundraiser page, video, and article ends at the email capture.
